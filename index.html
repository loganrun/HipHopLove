1973 The Birth of Hip Hop

DJ Kool Herc’s Back-To-School Jam

Kool Herc's back-to-school jam in 1973 is often hailed as the genesis of Hip Hop. In that sweltering August, Herc, a Jamaican immigrant, transformed his sister's block party with an innovative technique: using two turntables to isolate and extend the funky breakbeats of popular records. This "breakbeat juggling" kept the dancers moving long after the songs ended, sparking a dance revolution known as breakdancing.

But Hip Hop was more than just beats. Kool Herc's jams also featured MCs, charismatic figures who rhymed, toasted, and interacted with the crowd, adding another layer of energy and storytelling. These early MCs, like Coke La Rock and Grandmaster Flash, became the voices of the Bronx, weaving tales of daily life, struggles, and dreams while keeping the party rocking.

Kool Herc's backyard parties weren't just about entertainment; they were a community hub, a space for young people of color, predominantly Black and Latino, to express themselves in a society that often ignored or marginalized them. Hip Hop became their language, their art form, a way to claim their own narrative and find strength in unity.

Rapper's Delight - SugarHill Gang

The pulsating soundsystem in DJ Kool Herc's backyard may have birthed Hip Hop, but it was the Sugarhill Gang's 1979 smash hit "Rapper's Delight" that catapulted the genre onto the global stage. This unlikely trio – Michael "Sugarhill Mike" Garland, Henry "Big Bank Hank" Jackson, and Guy "Master Gee" O'Brien – transformed the raw energy of block parties into a chart-topping phenomenon, forever changing the musical landscape.

The story of "Rapper's Delight" is almost as legendary as the song itself. Sylvia Robinson, founder of Sugar Hill Records, stumbled upon the nascent genre while visiting her nephew Michael at a Bronx party. Impressed by the MCs captivating the crowd, she saw potential and invited Michael and his crew to the studio. What emerged was a groundbreaking track built on Chic's disco hit "Good Times", blending funky beats with Michael's rapid-fire rhymes about everyday life in the Bronx.

It was a gamble that paid off spectacularly. The infectious groove and Michael's storytelling rhymes resonated with listeners across the globe. "Rapper's Delight" cracked the Billboard Hot 100, reaching a then-unimaginable #36, and even higher on UK charts. It became a cultural phenomenon, captivating audiences with its playful swagger and introducing the world to Hip Hop's unique vocabulary and rhythms.
